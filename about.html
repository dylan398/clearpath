The Firm

Built on construction industry experience and a direct observation: most owners navigate the most consequential phase of their project without a single firm accountable for making it go right.

Pre-construction determines everything. Most owners go through it alone.

The decisions made before a permit is pulled — how the project is documented, which contractors are approached, how bids are structured, what gets compared against what — determine whether a project is built on budget, on schedule, and without the surprises that erode returns and relationships. Pre-construction is where projects are won or lost. It just doesn't look that way until construction is already underway and problems are visible.

Most owners manage this phase without professional support. They rely on their GC's sub list, take the architect's word on engineer coordination, and receive bids in whatever format each contractor chose to submit. By the time packages are being compared, the numbers can't be compared — different assumptions, different scope inclusions, different formats, different exclusions buried in attachments. The owner makes their decision anyway because the project needs to move. The problems that decision creates show up six months later.

Clearpath exists because that's a solvable problem. A managed pre-construction process — one where every design discipline is coordinated, every trade is properly solicited, and every bid is organized into a comparable package before the owner sees it — produces dramatically better outcomes. Better pricing, fewer surprises, cleaner contracts, and a construction phase that starts with a common understanding across every party involved.

We built this firm around the relationships and process required to deliver that outcome for any owner, on any project, at any scale. The network of architects, engineers, and contractors we work with was built deliberately over years of active project work in the DFW market. Those relationships are what make a managed process possible — and what allow us to consistently get competitive responses from contractors who know our standards and know what to expect when a Clearpath project comes across their desk.

Our Approach

How we think about pre-construction.

The Clearpath process isn't a checklist — it's a set of convictions about how pre-construction should be run, built from direct experience with how it fails when those convictions aren't applied.

01
Assessment before anything
Every engagement starts with a full project assessment before a single document is produced or a single contractor is contacted. This is not a formality — it's the phase where every assumption gets tested, every constraint gets surfaced, and the actual scope of what's required gets defined. Projects that skip this step or compress it tend to discover what was missed in the middle of construction, at a cost that's a multiple of what proper assessment would have required. We don't move to design until assessment is complete and documented.
02
Documentation is the foundation of pricing
A contractor can only price what they can see. Vague or incomplete documents produce vague or incomplete bids — and the owner pays for the ambiguity in the form of change orders, exclusion disputes, and rework during construction. We treat the documentation phase as the highest-leverage investment in a project's outcome. Getting documents right before solicitation — coordinated across all disciplines, reviewed for consistency, formatted for contractor use — is what produces comparable, complete bids that can actually be relied upon.
03
Solicitation is a direct activity, not a passive one
Posting a project and waiting for responses is not solicitation — it's hoping. Real solicitation means identifying specific contractors, making direct contact, following up by phone, managing questions through the bid period, and actively pursuing responses until adequate coverage in every trade is achieved. The difference in response rate between a managed solicitation and an email blast is significant and consistent. The difference in bid quality — how complete, how accurately priced, how responsive to the scope — is even larger.
04
Owners need options, not raw data
Presenting an owner with a stack of bids is not a deliverable. Bids in their raw form are incomparable by design — different formats, different inclusions, different assumptions, exclusions scattered throughout attachments. The work of turning raw bids into options an owner can actually choose between is where most of the pre-construction value is created, and it's the work that's most commonly skipped. We don't skip it. Every package we present has been reconciled, adjusted for scope gaps, and constructed to represent a coherent and comparable choice.
05
Committed means committed — not just approved in principle
A lot of pre-construction processes end with signed packages and numbers on paper, but without actual contractor commitments in place. The owner takes the package, tries to execute the contracts themselves, and discovers that the contractors have moved on to other projects, changed their pricing, or are no longer available on the timeline that was planned. We treat contractor commitment — executed contracts, confirmed mobilization dates, locked pricing — as part of our deliverable, not the owner's responsibility after handoff.
Operating Principles

What we hold ourselves to.

These aren't values statements — they're commitments that shape every decision we make on behalf of a client.

01
One accountable firm for the entire process
When we take on a project, we own the pre-construction process completely. That means we don't sub-delegate accountability. If a design professional misses a coordination issue, that's on us to catch and correct before it reaches bid documents. If a trade solicitation isn't producing adequate responses, it's on us to expand the effort and get them. If the assessment identified a permitting requirement that didn't make it into the documentation, we find it. The owner works with one firm, has one number to call, and holds one party accountable for the entire pre-construction outcome.
02
No conflicts of interest — ever
We have no financial relationship with any contractor, architect, engineer, or supplier we bring to a project. No referral fees, no preferred vendor arrangements, no commissions on contractor selections, no side agreements of any kind. The only money we receive is the flat fee we agree with our client before the engagement begins. This is not just a policy statement — it's the structural basis on which the entire Clearpath model rests. The value of what we do depends entirely on the owner being able to trust that every recommendation we make is made purely in their interest. The moment we have a financial stake in a contractor outcome, that trust is gone.
03
Relationships over platforms, every time
Bid platforms and online solicitation tools are useful for reaching a broad audience quickly. What they produce is volume, not quality — and in construction, the bids that come back from contractors who don't know the project, the market, or the standards required tend to reflect that. The contractors in our network know how we work. They know what level of documentation they'll receive. They know that questions get answered and that communication is organized. That familiarity produces better bid quality, more complete responses, and contractors who are genuinely committed when their bid is accepted — not contractors fishing for scope clarifications after award.
04
The deliverable is a ready-to-build project, not a set of options
Presenting package options to an owner is the second-to-last step, not the last one. The last step is taking the owner's selection and converting it into an executable project — executed contracts, finalized permits, confirmed mobilization schedules, and a complete handoff package. The distinction matters because many pre-construction processes stop at options. The owner receives a recommendation, says yes, and then has to go execute the contracts themselves — which involves another round of negotiations, delays, and the risk that committed pricing and availability have changed. We don't stop at options.
05
Honest assessments, even when uncomfortable
Part of working exclusively for the owner is telling them things they sometimes don't want to hear. If the budget doesn't support the scope, we say that in Phase 1 — before design work begins and before that fact becomes expensive. If a bid came in low because something is missing, we document what's missing and what the real cost is — we don't let an artificially low number stand because it looks good. If a contractor's references don't hold up, we tell the owner and explain what we found. The pre-construction process is only worth managing professionally if the owner receives honest information throughout. That's the only standard we hold ourselves to.
What We Are Not

Important distinctions.

Clearpath occupies a specific role in the construction process. Understanding what we're not is as important as understanding what we are.

Not This
A General Contractor
A GC builds the project. Their interest in the pre-construction process is in winning the contract and protecting their margin. They manage their own subs, on their own terms, with their own relationships — and those relationships have financial implications that don't always align with getting the owner the best value.
Clearpath manages pre-construction on the owner's behalf. We have no construction interest in the project and no financial stake in contractor selections.
Not This
A Bid Platform or Directory
Bid platforms aggregate contractor listings and allow project postings. They produce volume — sometimes a lot of it — but no coordination, no quality control, no follow-up, and no organization of what comes back. The owner still has to manage the process themselves once responses arrive.
Clearpath manages every step of solicitation directly — contact, follow-up, Q&A, organization — and delivers structured, comparable results.
Not This
A Broker or Referral Service
Some pre-construction consultants earn commissions or referral fees from the contractors they recommend. That arrangement — however disclosed — creates an incentive that runs counter to the owner's interest. A firm that earns more when a more expensive contractor is selected is not working for the owner.
Clearpath earns a flat fee agreed with the owner before engagement begins. We earn nothing from any contractor, under any arrangement.
Not This
An Advisory Service
Some consultants advise on pre-construction without owning the outcome. They review documents, attend meetings, and offer recommendations — but the actual work of coordinating design, soliciting contractors, and organizing bids stays with the owner or gets distributed across parties who aren't accountable to each other.
Clearpath owns and executes the full process. We don't advise — we deliver a complete, shovel-ready package.
