The Process

Five phases. Every engagement follows the same structured approach — scaled to what the project actually requires. A tenant improvement and a mixed-use development are different in scale, but the process that produces a sound, contractor-committed package is the same.

Every Engagement Starts Here

Project Assessment

Before we produce a single document or make a single call, we need a complete picture of what you're trying to build and what it will take to get there. That sounds straightforward — and it is, when it's done right. The problem is that most owners have never been through a formal pre-construction process, and don't always know which questions to answer or which details matter. Our assessment is designed to find all of it.

We'll walk through the site conditions, any existing documentation or design work, budget expectations, regulatory factors that will shape the design, the construction timeline, and anything else that will affect what the project requires and how it should be sequenced. At the end of this phase, we produce a written project brief that becomes the foundational document for everything that follows. Nothing moves forward until we've agreed on what we're building.

Getting assessment right is also how we protect you from scope creep. A project that's correctly defined at the start doesn't come back three months later with surprise requirements that weren't anticipated. That's a preventable problem, and this is where it gets prevented.

What Happens in This Phase
  • Owner brief — documented scope, objectives, constraints, and success criteria
  • Site review — conditions, access, topography, utilities, and existing infrastructure
  • Regulatory scan — zoning, permitting requirements, building codes, and environmental considerations
  • Budget parameters — what the project needs to come in at, and whether that's realistic
  • Trade identification — every discipline the project requires, from design through construction
  • Phasing plan — how the project sequences and what dependencies exist between trades
  • Written assessment summary — delivered and signed off before Phase 2 begins
02
Scaled to the Project

Design & Documentation

Every project needs documentation before a contractor can price it accurately. What that documentation looks like depends entirely on the project. Large commercial construction requires stamped architectural drawings, structural engineering, civil plans, MEP design, and detailed specifications. A parking lot rehabilitation requires a scope of work, site measurements, material specifications, and a defined bid form. We produce exactly what the project requires — using the appropriate professionals for the work at hand.

When a project calls for licensed architects or engineers, we bring them in through our established network of design professionals across DFW and North Texas. We have working relationships with architects, civil engineers, structural engineers, MEP engineers, land surveyors, geotechnical consultants, and specialty designers. We coordinate and manage their work, maintain the schedule, and ensure that every document produced is in the format contractors need to submit an accurate bid.

This is also where we catch problems before they become expensive. Incomplete or inconsistent design documents are one of the most common reasons bid prices vary wildly, contractors submit exclusions, and change orders multiply during construction. A complete document set, reviewed for coordination issues before it goes to bid, dramatically reduces all of those outcomes.

What This Phase Can Include
  • Architectural design coordination — schematic design through full construction documents as required
  • Structural, civil, and MEP engineering coordination for projects that require it
  • Geotechnical and environmental study coordination where site conditions warrant
  • Site plans, grading plans, utility layouts, drainage design
  • Scope of work documents and technical specifications for trade contractors
  • Permitting applications — preparation and submission to relevant jurisdictions
  • Document coordination review — verifying all disciplines are consistent before bid release
  • Bid document package assembly — all documents organized for contractor distribution
03
Every Trade. Real Responses.

Trade Solicitation

Once documentation is complete, we go to market — across every trade the project requires, simultaneously. General contractors, civil, structural, MEP, specialty subcontractors, suppliers where applicable — whoever the project needs. We don't manage one trade at a time or run sequential bid processes. Every discipline goes out on a common timeline so all responses come back in an organized window that allows for proper comparison.

Our approach to solicitation is direct and personal. We don't post to bid platforms and wait. We contact contractors we know, follow up by phone, and maintain those relationships project after project. That is what gets you four real bids instead of one, and what gets you responses from contractors who actually read the documents and price the full scope — not just the parts they felt like pricing.

We also manage all bidder communication during the solicitation period. Questions from any contractor are logged, answered, and issued as addenda to all bidders simultaneously. That keeps the process clean and prevents the informal "side conversations" that introduce scope inconsistencies into bid results.

What Happens in This Phase
  • Contractor identification — qualified, licensed, insured contractors in every required trade
  • Direct outreach — phone and email contact, not platform postings
  • Complete document package delivery to every bidder simultaneously
  • Pre-bid meeting coordination where project complexity warrants one
  • Bidder Q&A management — questions fielded, addenda issued to all parties
  • Active follow-up through the bid deadline — we chase responses
  • Bid receipt and logging — all responses timestamped and organized for packaging
  • Non-response documentation — who declined to bid and why
04
Options You Can Choose Between

Bid Organization & Packaging

Raw bids are data. What owners need is options — organized, comparable, and complete enough to support a real decision. This is the phase that separates a managed pre-construction process from an unmanaged one. We take every response across every trade and organize them into a small set of complete package options, typically structured as value, performance, and premium, so you're choosing between defined outcomes with all-in pricing — not trying to mix and match incompatible proposals.

Building each package requires judgment, not just spreadsheets. The value package isn't just "lowest price across every trade." It's the combination of contractors that produces the best overall project outcome at the most competitive total cost — which sometimes means paying more for one trade because a cheaper option for that trade would create risk or coordination problems elsewhere. We make those determinations with full context from the assessment and design phases, and we document our reasoning so you understand every choice.

Every package also goes through a scope reconciliation process before it's presented. Exclusions are identified and addressed. Scope gaps are quantified and added back into the pricing. Inconsistencies between bids are flagged and resolved. What you receive is packages where every number is apples-to-apples — not apparent packages where one number looks lower because something is missing.

What's In Each Delivered Package
  • Complete contractor roster — every required trade, with contractor profile for each selection
  • All-in pricing summary — fully loaded, no hidden exclusions or scope gaps
  • Scope reconciliation log — every discrepancy identified and resolved with supporting rationale
  • Contractor profiles — licensing, insurance status, bonding capacity, and relevant project history
  • Project timeline projection — realistic schedule from award through completion, per package
  • Risk summary — any notable risks or considerations specific to each package combination
  • Written analysis and recommendation — our assessment of each option with supporting reasoning
05
Shovel-Ready at Delivery

Approval & Handoff

Once you select a package, the pre-construction process isn't over — it's entered its most consequential stage. Getting from an approved package to a project with signed contracts, confirmed permits, and committed mobilization dates requires careful coordination across every party involved. This is where pre-construction processes that aren't actively managed fall apart, and where the most expensive delays get introduced.

We manage every step of the approval and commitment process. Selected contractors are notified and contract execution begins. Outstanding permits are tracked through to issuance. Pre-construction schedules are established with every trade to confirm mobilization dates, sequencing, and any lead time requirements for materials or equipment. Non-selected contractors are formally notified.

The handoff itself is a structured event. We don't email you a folder of documents and call it done. We walk through the complete project package with you — every contract, every permit, every pre-construction schedule — so that nothing is ambiguous when construction begins. By the time the project is in your hands, it is genuinely ready to execute.

What Happens Before Handoff
  • Contractor selection notifications — selected parties confirmed, non-selected formally notified
  • Contract execution management — we manage the process of getting every agreement fully executed
  • Insurance and bonding verification — certificates confirmed current before contracts are signed
  • Permit finalization — all outstanding applications tracked to issuance
  • Pre-construction schedule — confirmed mobilization dates and sequencing across all trades
  • Material and equipment lead time review — any long-lead items identified and ordered
  • Complete project package assembly — all documents organized in a single, structured handoff binder
  • Owner orientation — we review every element of the package with you before closing out

Executed Contracts

Permits & Approvals

Package Analysis & Recommendation
Our written analysis of all package options — including the option you selected and what was considered in building it — so you have a documented rationale for the decisions made and can reference it throughout the project.

Common Questions

Frequently asked.

Questions we hear often. If yours isn't here, reach out directly.

Do we need to have design documents or plans before contacting Clearpath? +
No. You don't need anything beyond a description of what you want to build. If you already have design documents or architectural drawings from a previous effort, we can absolutely work from those — but they're not a requirement. Phase 1 assessment starts with whatever information you have, and Phase 2 produces whatever documentation your project still needs. Many of our clients come to us with nothing more than a site and an idea.
How much does Clearpath charge? +
We charge a flat fee that's confirmed before any engagement begins. The fee varies based on project scale and complexity — what's required on a tenant improvement is different from what's required on a ground-up commercial development. We don't charge a percentage of the contract value, and our fee doesn't change based on how many trades are involved or how many bids come back. Contact us with your project details and we'll outline the process and fee specific to your project before you commit to anything.
What types of projects do you handle? +
Any project that has a pre-construction phase. Commercial construction, residential development, mixed-use, industrial, retail, hospitality, renovation and rehabilitation, site work and infrastructure. The process we run is the same regardless of project type — what changes is the design disciplines and trade contractors involved. If you're unsure whether your project fits, contact us and describe it. We'll tell you directly whether it's a good fit.
Can we engage Clearpath for only part of the process? +
Yes. The five phases are the complete engagement, but clients sometimes come to us with design documents already complete and need only solicitation, packaging, and handoff. Others have design and bids but need the bids organized into comparable packages. We can enter the process at any phase and take it forward from there. What we don't do is take partial ownership of a phase — if we're running solicitation, we run all of it, not part of it.
Do you work outside the DFW area? +
Our primary service area is the eight-county DFW Metroplex and immediate surrounding region, where our contractor network is strongest and we can respond most effectively. For larger projects — particularly those above $5M in total construction value — we work statewide. Our design professional relationships extend across Texas, and for larger scopes we can build out an appropriate contractor network for the specific location. Contact us with your project location and we'll confirm whether we can help.
How do you handle it if contractors don't respond to the solicitation? +
We follow up actively by phone throughout the solicitation period and document every contact. If we're not getting adequate response in a trade, we identify additional contractors and expand the solicitation before the bid deadline. Our goal for every trade is a minimum of three qualified responses — and when that's not achievable due to genuine market conditions, we tell you clearly, explain why, and present what we have with full context. We don't dress up an inadequate bid set as a competitive one.
Are the architects and engineers you use licensed in Texas? +
Yes. Every design professional we engage holds a current Texas license in their discipline and carries appropriate professional liability insurance. For projects requiring a Certificate of Occupancy or permit-ready drawings, documents are sealed by the licensed professional of record as required by the applicable jurisdiction. If a project has specific licensing requirements we haven't encountered before, we identify that in Phase 1 and confirm our network can address it before we proceed.
What information should we have ready before our first conversation? +
The more detail you can provide, the more useful the first conversation will be — but you don't need to have everything organized. Helpful to have: a description of what you want to build, the address or general location of the site, any existing documents (surveys, prior plans, permits), your budget range, and your desired timeline for construction to begin. If you have none of that, a clear description of the project in plain language is enough to start. We'll ask the questions we need from there.
